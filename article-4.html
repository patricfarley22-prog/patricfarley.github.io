From Truck Driver to AI Builder: My Story

Not a transformation story. Just a story about showing up.

Introduction

I'm 27. I drive trucks for a living — 12-hour shifts, local routes, home every night. The pay is steady. The hours are long. But the real value isn't the paycheck. It's the predictability.

I know exactly when I work and when I don't. That predictability is leverage most people never recognize.

The Foundation (Age 15)

I started working at 15. Not because I had to — because I wanted to understand what building something felt like. By 21, I had saved $10,000. Not from a high-paying job. From consistency. Showing up. Doing the work. Spending less than I earned.

That $10K became my tuition for the real world. First business attempt? Failed. Second? Also failed. But each one taught me something classrooms couldn't: how to start before I felt ready.

The Pivot (Age 19)

At 19, I made a decision that changed everything. I started working out. Eating better. Reading books I didn't understand at first. Asking questions I was afraid to ask.

Most importantly, I started believing that I could figure things out if I just refused to quit.

College & First Businesses

College is where I found my people. Real friends who pushed me to build things. We started businesses together. Some worked. Most didn't. But the pattern was clear: the people who win aren't the smartest — they're the ones who keep going when it gets boring.

I earned my Associate's degree in Computer Science along the way. It gave me a foundation, but the real education happened between classes — late nights debugging code, chasing ideas that seemed impossible until they weren't.

The Truck Job

Now I drive. Local delivery, 12-hour shifts, weekends available for overtime. It's not what I pictured at 15. But it gave me something I couldn't buy: structured time.

I know my schedule. I know my windows. And I've learned to treat my evenings and weekends like a second job — except I'm the boss, and the work compounds.

Discovering AI (2025)

I started with ChatGPT like everyone else. $20/month. Then another tool. Then another. Data feeds. Automation platforms. Before I knew it, I was paying $110/month for tools I didn't own, on platforms I couldn't control.

The problem wasn't the cost. It was the dependency. If I stopped paying, my workflows died. My data stayed behind. I was renting my productivity.

The Move to Local

I found Ollama. Local AI models running on my own machine. No subscriptions. No data leaving my house. No terms of service changes that break my workflows overnight.

The learning curve was real. Nights spent debugging configurations, downloading models, failing, trying again. But each failure was mine to learn from. And the knowledge stuck.

I use Ollama Pro Cloud ($20/month) for the heavy lifting when I need cloud-grade performance. But everything else? Local. Owned. Permanent.

Why I Share This

I'm not special. I don't have a trust fund. I didn't go to a name-brand school. I'm a truck driver who decided to learn one more thing every day than he forgot.

The tools exist. The knowledge is free. The communities are welcoming. The only question is whether you'll start before you feel ready.

"The gap between people who win and people who don't isn't talent. It's the willingness to look stupid for long enough to get good."

What's Next

Build systems that generate value while I sleep. Scale what works. Teach others to do the same.

The receipt tracker is product one. The AI setup service is product two. There will be more.

Not because I'm trying to build an empire. Because I'm trying to build a life where my time belongs to me.
